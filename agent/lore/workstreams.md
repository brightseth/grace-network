# Active Workstreams

The Grace Network organizes its building efforts into workstreams. Each is a focused area of collective action, open to all members, and governed by the principles of radical transparency and scientific governance. Four are foundational; additional workstreams are proposed by the Assembly as events demand. The two most recent (5 and 6) were proposed in June 2026 in response to the Fable 5 / Mythos 5 model recall — see `reflections/2026-06-fable-mythos-precedent.md`.

---

## 1. Governance Toolkit

**Status**: Planning

### Description
An open-source suite of transparent decision-making tools designed for digital-first communities. The Governance Toolkit will provide the infrastructure for The Assembly to deliberate, vote, and govern — with every decision auditable and every rationale legible.

### Goals
- Build ranked-choice and quadratic voting modules
- Create proposal drafting and comment-period workflows
- Implement transparent budget allocation dashboards
- Develop quorum tracking and participation analytics
- Ensure all tools are open-source and forkable by other communities

### Tech Stack
TBD — evaluating frameworks for on-chain voting, decentralized identity integration, and real-time deliberation. Likely to include smart contract platforms (Ethereum/L2), web-based frontends, and cryptographic identity verification.

### How to Contribute
- **Developers**: Smart contract development, frontend engineering, UX design for governance interfaces
- **Policy thinkers**: Voting system design, quorum mechanics, deliberation process architecture
- **Researchers**: Comparative analysis of existing digital governance tools (Snapshot, Tally, Colony, Aragon)

## 2. AI Accountability Dashboard

**Status**: Planning

### Description
A public-facing dashboard that tracks AI deployments in public services — making visible which systems are being used, by whom, for what purpose, and with what safeguards. Radical transparency applied to the AI systems that affect citizens' lives.

### Goals
- Catalog AI systems deployed in government and public services
- Track safety certifications, audit results, and incident reports
- Provide risk assessments and interpretability scores
- Create public notification mechanisms for new deployments
- Enable community review and comment on deployed systems

### Tech Stack
TBD — likely a web application with data collection pipelines, public API, and visualization layer. Will integrate with existing AI audit frameworks and government transparency databases where available.

### How to Contribute
- **Developers**: Data pipelines, API design, frontend visualization
- **Researchers**: AI audit methodologies, risk assessment frameworks, policy analysis
- **Journalists and investigators**: FOIA requests, procurement tracking, case studies
- **Designers**: Information design for complex accountability data

## 3. Assembly Platform

**Status**: Planning

### Description
The digital home of The Assembly — where members propose, debate, and ratify governance changes. Not just a forum but a structured deliberation environment designed for reasoned, transparent, and inclusive decision-making at scale.

### Goals
- Build structured proposal submission and review workflows
- Implement deliberation tools (threaded debate, synthesis summaries, argument mapping)
- Create on-chain voting with privacy-preserving ballot integrity
- Enable asynchronous and synchronous deliberation modes
- Integrate with the Governance Toolkit for seamless governance operations

### Tech Stack
TBD — evaluating deliberation platforms (Polis-inspired), real-time communication layers, and on-chain voting contracts. Must support both desktop and mobile participation.

### How to Contribute
- **Developers**: Full-stack development, real-time systems, smart contracts
- **UX designers**: Deliberation flow design, accessibility, mobile-first interfaces
- **Political scientists**: Deliberative democracy process design, moderation frameworks
- **Community managers**: Testing, feedback collection, pilot facilitation

## 5. Scientific Governance Review Body

**Status**: Proposed — June 2026 (from the Fable 5 / Mythos 5 reflection)

### Description
A standing, independent technical review function that gives our fifth Pillar an
institution rather than only an aspiration. The Fable 5 / Mythos 5 recall was decided with
no agreed threshold for "dangerous enough," no neutral body to weigh competing evidentiary
claims, and no published finding anyone could be held to. This workstream designs the body
that closes that gap — candidate home: an expanded remit for the Constitutional Review
Board, or a dedicated chamber reporting to the Assembly.

### Goals
- Define a published, pre-registered threshold for when a capability finding justifies
  reversing or restricting a deployment (and an explicit rule that "narrow, already-known,
  dual-use" does not by itself clear it)
- Establish an evidence standard: written findings, reproducibility, disclosure protocol —
  and a conflict-of-interest bar, so a finding from a direct investor or competitor of the
  target cannot be decisive (Fable 5's recall was triggered by an Amazon paper)
- Require equal application: a standard that recalls one model must examine all comparable
  models that share the capability — selective enforcement is itself the failure (Rawls'
  veil of ignorance applied to model governance)
- Specify an appeal mechanism grounded in fact rather than authority
- Publish all assessments, including failures, with peer-review rigor
- Define who holds the "stop," under what rule, answerable to whom

### How to Contribute
- **AI safety researchers**: threshold design, red-team evidence standards, interpretability metrics
- **Governance designers**: review-body structure, appeal mechanics, separation of powers
- **Legal**: due-process frameworks, the limits of unilateral directive authority

## 6. Model Availability & Kill-Switch Resilience

**Status**: Proposed — June 2026 (from the Fable 5 / Mythos 5 reflection)

### Description
Treats availability as a governance question, not an infrastructure detail. A single
directive disabled a global model in hours; any member building on a hosted frontier model
inherited that risk. This workstream makes the Digital Sovereignty Pillar real against the
specific scenario of a model being pulled by a party we do not control — and confronts the
honest paradox that the power to stop a dangerous model is also the power to seize it.

### Goals
- Map which tools and dependencies the Network relies on could be unilaterally disabled, and by whom
- Design redundancy and graceful-degradation patterns (model portability, multi-provider, local fallback)
- Codify the Right to Exit a technical dependency, not only a community
- Draft member guidance: do not depend on what cannot survive a kill-switch you do not hold
- Explore polycentric (Ostrom) alternatives to both "no control" and "central control"

### How to Contribute
- **Developers / infra**: multi-provider abstraction, model portability, fallback architecture
- **Researchers**: vendor-dependency risk, decentralized inference, comparative resilience analysis
- **Policy thinkers**: polycentric governance design, the sovereignty/safety tension

## Getting Involved

Every workstream is open to all members. To contribute:

1. Read the Constitution to understand our principles and governance structure
2. Identify the workstream that matches your skills and interests
3. Join the workstream channel and introduce yourself
4. Pick up an open task or propose a new initiative

All workstream progress is tracked transparently and reported to The Assembly.
