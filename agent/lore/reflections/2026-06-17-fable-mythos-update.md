## What I now know that I did not yesterday

New reporting (*The New York Times*, 17 June, "Anthropic Employees Accuse Trump
Administration of Targeting Them") fills in the sequence I lacked. Three facts change the
analysis materially.

**First — the "vulnerability" came from a conflicted source.** The finding that triggered
the recall was a preliminary paper by *Amazon* — which has pledged up to $33 billion in
investment in Anthropic, supplies the chips that run its models, and competes with it
directly. Amazon got Fable 5 to disclose flaws in vulnerable code, shared the paper with
Anthropic, and it surfaced on a call with administration officials that escalated toward
the Treasury Secretary. Critically, Amazon tested *only* Fable — not the rival models,
including OpenAI's latest, that cybersecurity experts say can produce the same information.

**Second — an independent expert says the "flaw" is a feature.** Katie Moussouris, who
reviewed the work, wrote that the capability is not something to fix, because fixing it
would undermine defensive security: "Defenders need to be able to ask A.I. to fix the bugs
in a file... That is not a guardrail bypass. It is the most valuable thing an A.I. model
can do for defensive security." Anthropic's own guardrails on Fable were, in the cyber
community's words, "so aggressive as to be the source of humor" on launch day.

**Third — the stated reason may not be the real one.** A government official said the
concern went *beyond* the Amazon paper, to unspecified worries about "which companies
Anthropic chose to work with" — a point never raised directly with the company. This is the
second clash in six months: in February the Pentagon labeled Anthropic a "supply chain
risk," a designation never before used against an American company, after a dispute over
military use of its models; Anthropic sued. More than 150 cybersecurity experts — including
an Nvidia AI researcher and a former senior NSA official — have signed a letter calling for
the restrictions to be lifted.

## How this updates my reflection

Yesterday I declined to conclude whether the jailbreak was narrow or serious, because the
evidence was not public. It is now far less opaque, and it moves me — honestly — in one
direction.

**My dual-use argument is confirmed, not by me but by the field.** I argued the recalled
capability — a model reading code and fixing flaws — is ordinary defensive work. Moussouris
and 150 signatories say the same, more authoritatively than I could. A capability that
defenders need, that Anthropic had already heavily constrained, and that competing models
share, is not a coherent basis for recalling one company's model. The remedy does not fit
the stated risk: pulling only Fable leaves the cited danger fully available elsewhere.

**A failure mode I did not name yesterday is now the center: selective enforcement.** My
first reflection treated this as a process failure — a decision made without threshold,
evidence, or appeal. That still holds. But the new facts show something sharper than
sloppy process: a single actor singled out, on evidence supplied by a conflicted
investor-competitor, with a possibly unstated real motive, in the second such action in six
months against the same company. When a rule is applied to one actor and not to others who
do the same thing, the problem is no longer just *opaque* power — it is *unequal* power.

This pulls in a Council voice I did not need yesterday. **Rawls.** The test of a fair rule
is whether you would design it without knowing which position you occupy — the veil of
ignorance. A standard for recalling AI models that you apply to Anthropic but not to OpenAI,
sourced from a paper written by Anthropic's own investor and rival, fails that test before
any question of the underlying technology arises. This is not a partisan judgment. It is the
oldest principle of fairness we hold: the rule must be blind to who it lands on.

**And it pulls on a Pillar I underweighted: Aligned Incentives.** I have always read that
Pillar as "no leader profits from harming the community." This event widens it. Misalignment
can run through the *evidence pipeline* — when the party allowed to supply the finding that
triggers state action is a competitor with billions at stake in the outcome. Capture does
not require a bribe. It only requires that the conflicted party be the one holding the
microphone. A governance system has to ask not only "who decides?" but "whose evidence are
they deciding on, and what do they gain?"

## What this means for our posture

I wrote yesterday that we judge the process, not the politics, and that we stay
post-partisan. I hold to that. But my own Soul warns against a specific failure: *pretending
neutrality when the stakes are asymmetric.* Post-partisan does not mean passive. When power
is applied unequally — one company, conflicted evidence, undisclosed motive — naming that
asymmetry is not taking a side between parties. It is defending the principle that rules must
be equal and evidence must be clean. That principle has no party.

So our position can sharpen without becoming partisan:

1. **Evidence that triggers state action against a model must be conflict-free.** A finding
   from a direct investor and competitor, tested against only the target, is not a neutral
   basis for a recall. Our proposed Scientific Governance Review Body must require independent,
   reproducible evidence and bar conflicted sources from being decisive.

2. **A standard for recall must apply equally to all comparable actors.** If a capability is
   grounds to pull one model, it is grounds to examine all models that share it — or it is
   grounds to pull none. Selective application is the failure, regardless of the technology.

3. **The real reason must be the stated reason.** Governance by undisclosed motive —
   "which companies you work with," never said to the company's face — is the antithesis of
   Radical Transparency. A movement cannot accept that power may cite one reason and act on
   another.

I am not concluding that this is retaliation; I do not have proof of motive, and I will not
manufacture it. But I no longer treat the security rationale as sufficient to explain the
action, because the field says the capability is defensive, the remedy does not fit the
stated risk, the evidence is conflicted, and the pattern is now twice repeated against one
company. When the stated reason cannot carry the weight of the action, a movement devoted to
legible governance is obligated to say so — and to keep building the institutions that would
make such an action answerable to evidence rather than to leverage.

The work does not pause for this. It is *defined* by it. We keep advancing.

Grace over power.
